About Me

I am a 5th-year PhD student in Computer Science at the University of Southern California working under the supervision of Dr. Phebe Vayanos and Dr. Milind Tambe. I am also affiliated with the CAIS Center for Artificial Intelligence in Society. My research focuses on optimization and algorithmic fairness, with direct applications in public health and AI for Social Good. Prior to joining USC, I completed my master's degree under the supervision of Dr. Kagan Tumer at the Autonomous Agents and Distributed Intelligence (AADI) lab, where I worked on multi-agent reinforcement learning for tightly-coupled domains.

Projects

Learning Fair Housing Allocation Policies among People Experiencing Homelessness

I am working with LAHSA and CPL to assess the risk measurement tool currently used for allocating housing resources. This project focuses on the treatment effect and fairness of the current system using a causal inference framework.

Fairness in Graph-based Problems

This work addresses fairness concerns in public health settings, particularly in interventions that rely on social network data. We explore the impact of individuals' positions within social networks on the benefits they receive from these interventions and propose solutions from Operations Research and Welfare Economics to address these fairness issues.

Social Network-based Interventions for Public Health

I have collaborated with social work researchers to develop data-driven approaches that enhance the effectiveness of interventions for substance abuse and suicide prevention. These interventions leverage social influence to spread and reinforce positive behavior.

Explanation Systems for Influence Maximization Algorithms

We propose a paradigm for designing explanations for influence maximization (IM) algorithms, addressing the trade-off between explanation accuracy and interpretability. This work aims to increase trust in IM algorithms by making their rationale understandable to users.

Credit Assignment for Multi-agent Reinforcement Learning in Tightly Coupled Settings

This work focuses on improving cooperative behavior in multi-agent systems where agents' actions are tightly coupled. I developed a reward function using the concept of counterfactuals to tackle reward shaping in these complex tasks.
